Frequently Asked Questions

Got a question? We've got answers. If you have some other questions, see our support center.

How well is Germany prepared?
Germany is as well prepared as it possibly can be. We have a network of centres of excellence and specialist clinics in Germany that is unmatched anywhere in the world. We have a very good disease warning and notification system, as well as excellent pandemic preparedness plans. The National Pandemic Preparedness Plan is the basis for pandemic planning in Germany. The plan was adopted in 2017 by the Federation and the federal states and is updated regularly. In addition, regular emergency training exercises are carried out at Germany’s airports. The Robert Koch Institute is responsible for coordination and information.
What emergency plans are in place?
The Federal Ministry of Health has a National Pandemic Preparedness Plan, drawn up with the Robert Koch Institute, one of its executive agencies. This is implemented by the federal states in their own pandemic preparedness plans.
Does Germany have enough protective equipment?
The crisis task force has decided that there is an emergency situation in the procurement of protective medical equipment and in capacity in intensive care units. The Federal Ministry of Health procures such equipment centrally for medical practices, hospitals and federal authorities. The Procurement Office of the Federal Ministry of the Interior helps with large-scale purchases of disinfectant; the Federal Agency for Technical Relief is in charge of distributing protective clothing and equipment to the federal authorities.
Which Federal Government bodies are dealing with the coronavirus pandemic?
Within the division of responsibilities in Germany’s federal system, in case of a pandemic the Federation plays a coordinating role, promoting a unified approach to fighting the pandemic and running public information campaigns, as well as making recommendations to the federal states. In addition to the regular Wednesday meetings of the Federal Cabinet, the Federal Chancellor also meets on Mondays with the federal ministers of finance, foreign affairs, health, defence and the interior, and on Thursdays with all federal ministers responsible for the specific areas to be discussed. These groups assess the current situation, identify the general thrust of actions within the federal sphere of responsibility and monitor processes critical to success during the crisis. The joint crisis task force of the Federal Ministry of Health and the Federal Ministry of the Interior addresses operational issues and takes immediate action, for example implementing decisions made in the Federal Chancellor’s Monday and Thursday meetings, responding to requests from the federal states for assistance, and collecting and preparing data for situation reports. In addition to the federal ministries of health and the interior, representatives of the Federal Chancellery, the Federal Foreign Office, the Federal Ministry of Defence, the Federal Ministry for Economic Affairs and Energy and the Federal Ministry of Labour and Social Affairs also work with the joint crisis task force. It currently meets twice weekly (Tuesdays and Thursdays). In line with the plan drawn up for this type of situation, the crisis task force is headed jointly by the Federal Ministry of the Interior (State Secretary Hans-Georg Engelke) and the Federal Ministry of Health (State Secretary Dr Thomas Steffen).
